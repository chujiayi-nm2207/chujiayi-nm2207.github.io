This is John

John has a mediocre GPA as compared to his peers. He’s a committee member in his faculty club and while he enjoys what he is doing, he is unsure if this is his passion and if he wants to pursue this career. He’s also 2.5 years into his University studies and thinks it’s too late to change his field of studies.

Does John sound like you?

Let’s take a look at job satisfaction across the workforce in Singapore.

A study across Southeast Asia has shown that the Singaporean workforce has the worst mental health amongst the countries, with workers claiming to have the lowest levels of engagement, job satisfaction and overall quality of life.

Only one in three Singaporeans say they ‘always’ or ‘nearly always’ look forward to going to work every morning, according to Qualtrics.

Two in five said they are not motivated to go to work every day.

Despite the low job satisfaction, why are people still in their jobs?

Salary is actually not the key driver of satisfaction among Singaporean employees. In fact, employees who earn salaries of under $30,000 annually have rated their job satisfaction at 48%, which is just two percent lower than the highest paid employees earning $130,000 and above, as found by a report.

Prior to Covid-19, many employees who were dissatisfied with their jobs just chose to stay with it, in fear of having gaps in their CV and being labelled a “job hopper” if they decided to change jobs. (CNA,2022)

Companies are also less likely to hire those who looked like they job hop, knowing that they might soon leave for a new job again. (CNA,2022)

However, companies have gradually realised that making judgements and labelling people as job hoppers are not serving anyone’s best interest. Perhaps by inquiring a little deeper into someone’s reason for job hopping, employers can learn more about potential employees and discover valuable skills and knowledge they can bring into the company. (CNA,2022)

According to the survey among 500 Singaporean jobseekers, primary reason why they would leave the organisation:

People who job hop tend to be perceived negatively because of…

association with loyalty

disability to adapt

not being serious

Do take note: While most employers are willing to consider candidates who have multiple roles of less than two years, they will still ask tough questions during the hiring process to ascertain if this person is worth investing in. (CNA, 2022)

Alternatively, here's how you can portray yourself to potential employers since a recent Milieu Insight survey suggests job hoppers aren’t seen negatively in Singapore. Employers may be becoming more open-minded about people moving from company to company, especially in these fluid times where restructuring can be vital for continued success. (CNA, 2022)

In start-ups or in sectors like marketing and finance, it can be the culture to change firms, say after about two years so it is natural to link career changes to growth and exposure. Professionals have also derived that by staying too long in certain sectors, people wonder why you aren’t exposing yourself to more experiences or they may think that you can’t get a better offer anywhere else. (CNA,2022)

Some may perceive job hoppers as introducing diverse experiences to the company, being more adaptable and having the ability to learn faster in a foreign environment. (CNA, 2022)

The original job scope may no longer be available due to sector transformation, restructuring and increasing digitalisation. (CNA, 2022)

Another reason for a change in career path may be, especially with Covid-19, sabbaticals and career experiments such as micro-entrepreneurship and home businesses. (CNA, 2022)

By framing and offering your career path in a way that relates to growth and exposure, you can showcase your unique skills, knowledge, competencies and experiences that employers deem valuable. Employers are also more forgiving and open to the idea of job hopping in the early stages of a career, linking it to personal discoveries of what individuals want and do not want.

Hence, as an undergraduate, you can still re-consider your career choices.
